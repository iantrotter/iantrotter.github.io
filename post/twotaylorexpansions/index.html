Two Incredibly Useful Taylor Expansions

Ian Michael Trotter
Mar 20, 2018

A “Taylor expansions” is a polynomial that approximates a function in a small vicinity of a single point. They are exceptionally useful: in general, we are pretty good at solving problems involving polynomials and pretty rubbish at solving problems involving pretty much anything else. Here, we show you two elegant Taylor expansions that keep showing up here and there in different areas of research.

In many dynamic systems, equations or functions you often find a dampening term that looks something like this: $$ e^{-\rho t}, \rho > 0 $$ This is a standard way of including dampening in any dynamical system: the greater the time that passes ($t$), the smaller the term $e^{-\rho t}$, and when multiplying it with some other factor, it makes the factor fade off into zero.

When $x$ is close to zero, we can use a simple trick to get an intuitive feel for the value of $e^{x}$. First, note that $e^0 = 1$. Secondly, recall that the derivative of $e^{x}$ with respect to $x$ is simply $e^{x}$. We will se these two observations to build a Taylor expansion of $e^{x}$ around $x_0=0$.

The general form of the first-degree Taylor expansion of a general function $f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}$ around a point $x_0$ is the following: $$ f(x_0 + \Delta x) \approx f(x_0) + f’(x_0) \Delta x. $$ Using the function $f(x) = e^{x}$ and constructing a Taylor expansion for $e^x$ around $x_0=0$, we find the following: $$ e^{x_0+x} \approx e^{x_0} + e^{x_0}x. $$ From which – since $x_0=0$ – we can simply see that: $$ e^x \approx 1 + x. $$

This is an incredibly elegant and powerful approximation: when $x$ is small, we can approximate $e^x$ by simply $1+x$. So if you see something like $e^{-0.03t}$ and start wondering what that actually means, just remember that you can approximate $e^{-0.03}$ simply by $1-0.03=0.97$. That is, it is a very slow decay, with about 97% of the original amount remaining after each period.

For those who want greater accuracy, you can easily use a higher degree Taylor expansion. However, this has been left as an exercise for the curious reader.

I promised two amazing Taylor expansions, so here’s the other one: $$ \ln(1+x) \approx x$$ Huh - what?

Suppose that you want to know the approximate value of $\ln(\cdot)$ at a point that is close to $1$. WE know that $\ln(1)=0$ and that the derivative of $\ln(x)$ is simply $1/x$, so the Taylor expansion can be written like this: $$ \ln(1+x) \approx \ln(1) + \frac{1}{1}x $$ Or in other words: $$ \ln(1+x) \approx x$$

This is also one of those functions that keep popping up when you’re playing around with applied mathematics, so it’s nice to have such a simple approximation in order to get an intuitive feel of what you’re working with. Those who read my post on the rule of 72 will know what I’m talking about.

With these two approximations fresh in mind, go forth and approximate!

Sometimes we can solve a problem almost correctly in a small fraction of the time it takes to solve the problem exactly.
